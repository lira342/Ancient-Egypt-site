EGYPTIAN MYTHOLOGY

"From the Dark Waters of Nun to the Eternal Sky of Nut"

Explore the ancient beliefs that shaped one of history's greatest civilizations — a world where gods walked among mortals and magic flowed through the Nile.

The Creation Myth

To the Egyptians, the creation of the world and universe came out of darkness. According to them, nothing existed except for endless dark waters known as Nun.

Heka, the god of magic and medicine, is believed to have already existed by this time and awaited the creation of all things. From these dark waters rose a primordial hill known as Ben-Ben, which the god Atum stood over.

Atum, seeing the nothingness of the dark waters and out of his own loneliness, used magic to mate with a shadow of himself and gave birth to two children. Whether he or his shadow gave birth to them is unclear, but it is said the first one, Shu, the god of air, he spat out, and the second one, Tefnut, the goddess of moisture, he vomited out.

Shu is said to have given the world the principles of life, while Tefnut gave the world the principles of order.

The two sibling gods left their father to establish the world. After a long time, Atum grew worried and removed his eye, sending it out in search of his children. As he awaited their return, he contemplated the concept of eternity.

When his children returned with his eye, he shed tears of joy, and these tears landed on the fertile primordial hill and gave birth to human beings.

Shu and Tefnut mated and gave birth to Geb (the Earth) and Nut (the Sky), so that humans had a place to live. The siblings fell deeply in love, which Atum found unacceptable. He pushed Nut high up into the heavens and Geb remained below — two lovers who could see each other but never touch.

Atum had parted them too late, however, as Nut was already impregnated by Geb. She eventually gave birth to Osiris, Isis, Set, Horus, and Nephthys — the most commonly known gods within the Egyptian pantheon.

The Pantheon of Gods

Amun

Supreme Creator God of All Creation
Amun rose out of a primordial hill, and recognizing how lonely he was and the nothingness all around him, created the universe. He is the father of all the gods.

Upon the reunification of Upper and Lower Egypt, Ahmose I reinvented Amun as Amun-Ra, combining the local patron god Amun with the sun god Ra of Lower Egypt to achieve both theological and political legitimacy.

Depicted as a bearded man wearing a headdress with two tall plumes. In the New Kingdom, represented as a ram-headed man.

Heka

God of Magic and Medicine
Heka is said to have existed before Atum and was the mystical force behind creation itself, used by Atum to bring the world into existence.

Most of what is known about Heka comes from Egyptian medical texts, spells, and incantations. He is also seen in funerary inscriptions meant to guide the soul in the afterlife.

Depicted as a man in royal dress, wearing the regal curved beard of the gods and carrying a staff entwined with two serpents - a symbol later adopted by the Greeks for Asclepius and now the symbol of the medical profession.

Osiris

Lord and Judge of the Dead
His name in ancient Egyptian means "Powerful" or "Mighty." Birthed by Nut the sky goddess, he was later killed by his brother Set, then resurrected by his wife Isis, known as the "Mother of the Gods."

He fathered Horus and went into the underworld to serve as Judge of the Dead. According to the Book of the Dead, he weighs the hearts of the dead against the white feather of Ma'at - the Feather of Truth.

Depicted as a mummy with green or black skin, symbolizing the fertility of the Nile region.

Horus

God of the Sky, Sun and Divine Kingship
There are two Horuses in Egyptian mythology: Horus the Elder (one of five gods born from Nut) and Horus the Younger, the more popular figure, son of Osiris and Isis after Osiris' resurrection.

After Set murdered Osiris, Isis resurrected him and conceived Horus. Horus and his mother fled to the Delta swamps, where he was raised. When he came of age, he avenged his father's murder and defeated Set, becoming ruler of all Egypt.

Most kings of Egypt linked themselves to Horus in life and Osiris in death, depicting themselves as the living incarnation of Horus.

Armor & Regalia

The Kepresh

Royal Battle Crown

Material: Stiffened leather layered with cloth dyed blue

Decoration: Golden discs (studded)

Purpose: Authority, military prowess, divine protection

Symbolism & Authority

The Kepresh was worn by Pharaohs into battle and symbolized authority, military prowess, and divine protection. It served as a visible manifestation of the king's dual role as both military leader and divine intermediary.

Construction & Materials

The headgear was crafted from stiffened leather and layered with cloth dyed blue — a color associated with the heavens and divinity. It was often studded with golden discs, though some variations existed without them.

Psychological Warfare

Although it offered some physical protection, the Kepresh served primarily for psychological impact against enemies on the battlefield. Its distinctive appearance made the Pharaoh easily identifiable, commanding both awe and fear.

Artistic Elements

The use of blue dye references the celestial realm of the gods, while the golden discs represent the sun (Ra) and divine radiance. The stiffened structure creates imposing vertical lines, emphasizing the Pharaoh's elevated status above mortal men.

Impact on Society

Daily Life

Egyptian mythology permeated every aspect of daily existence. From morning rituals honoring Ra's journey across the sky to household shrines for protective deities like Bes, religious practice was not confined to temples. The concept of Ma'at (truth, balance, and order) guided moral behavior, social interactions, and legal proceedings.

Trade & Economy

Religious beliefs directly influenced trade networks. Temples served as economic centers, managing vast agricultural estates. The demand for incense, myrrh, and gold for ritual purposes drove extensive trade routes through Nubia, Punt, and the Levant. Funerary goods created entire industries of craftsmen, from papyrus makers to canopic jar artisans.

Politics & Kingship

The Pharaoh was considered the living embodiment of Horus, maintaining Ma'at against the forces of chaos (Isfet). Political legitimacy derived directly from theological endorsement — as seen when Ahmose I merged Amun with Ra to unify a divided kingdom. Military campaigns were framed as cosmic battles between order and chaos, with the king as divine champion.

Art & Architecture

Artistic conventions were governed by religious symbolism rather than realism. Scale indicated importance, not perspective. Temples were designed as cosmic models, with pylons representing the horizon and sanctuary chambers as the womb of creation. The prohibition against depicting certain gods in specific contexts shaped the evolution of Egyptian artistic tradition for millennia.
