Egyptian mythology had a heavy influence on every aspect of the daily life of citizens and the political sphere of Egyptian civilization. Egyptian mythology also heavily influenced other cultures such as the Greek and Roman civilizations, as they borrowed from the Egyptian pantheon of gods and made them their own. Egypt also borrowed from these cultures as well, the development of the god Zeus- Amon which Alexander the Great declared himself to be the son of upon his conquest of Egypt. (an example of syncretism. Use this paragraph on syncretism) 

According to Joshua J. Mark, the central theme in Egyptian mythology was the belief in an eternal life after death, and the preparation thereof. In her book, The Encyclopaedia of Ancient Egypt, historian Margaret Bunson writes that eternity was,the goal and destiny of all human life in Egyptian religious beliefs, a stage of existence in which mortals could attain everlasting bliss.

Born of Egypt’s great god Amen (personified by King Seti I) and Queen Tuya, Ramses (RAM-zeez) was designated “while yet in the egg” as Egypt’s future king: Such is Ramses II’s account of his own birth. The period into which he was born, that of the New Kingdom, was a time when Egypt was attempting to maintain control of an extensive empire that ranged from the Fourth Cataract of the Nile in the Sudan to the provinces of North Syria. Some fifty years prior to his birth, during the Eighteenth Dynasty, Egypt had undergone a period of turmoil. Akhenaton (Amenhotep IV, r. 1377–1360 B.C.E.), reacting against the ever-growing power of the Amen priesthood, had abandoned the traditional religion and proclaimed the sun god, represented as the sun disk Aton, as sole god of the country. He worshiped the Aton at the virgin site of Amarna. He died without heirs; after his demise, a series of relatively ineffectual kings, including Tutankhamen (r. 1361–1352 B.C.E.), ruled for brief periods as the Amen priesthood set about reestablishing religious domination and refurbishing Amen’s temples. Meanwhile, using this period of uncertainty in Egypt to best advantage, vassal states in Syria held back their tribute and fomented revolt. When Tutankhamen died without living heirs, a military man of nonroyal birth, Ay, assumed the throne. He was followed only four years later by another, the general Horemheb, who also ruled only a short period before his death, but not before designating another man with a military background, his vizier Pa-Ramessu, as his heir. Pa-Ramessu (Ramses I, first king of the Nineteenth Dynasty) had what rulers since Akhenaton had lacked: viable male descendants. Thus, when Ramses I died after only a two-year rule, his son, Seti I (r. 1294–1279 B.C.E.), assumed the throne. Immediately, Seti began an active program of military campaigns in Canaan, Syria, and Libya. During many of these excursions, his son, the young Ramses II, was at his side, learning the art of warfare. Seti I instructed his son in civil and religious affairs as well, and Prince Ramses accompanied his father or acted as his deputy on state occasions and at religious festivals. As prince-regent, Ramses received the rights of Egyptian kingship, including his titulary (five royal names attributing to him divine power and linking him with Egypt’s divine past) and a harem.

A book about Ramesses III, also known as Ramesses the Great, explores the life of one of Ancient Egypt’s most powerful and celebrated pharaohs. Ruling for over 66 years during the Nineteenth Dynasty, Ramesses II is remembered for his military campaigns, extensive building projects, and strong leadership. The book examines his famous victory claims at the Battle of Kadesh, the construction of magnificent temples such as Abu Simbel, and his efforts to strengthen Egypt’s economy and influence. Through archaeological discoveries, ancient inscriptions, and historical records, readers gain a deeper understanding of his achievements and why he is regarded as one of the greatest rulers in Egyptian history. Reigned (1187–57 BCE) Ruled during the 20th Dynasty from about 1186 BCE to 1155 BCE. (for about 31 years) Referred to as the last great pharaoh of the New Kingdom because he successfully defended Egypt from powerful enemies and continued its rich traditions. son of Pharaoh Setnakhte, the founder of the 20th Dynasty. He became king after his father's death and inherited a kingdom that faced many threats from foreign invaders and economic difficulties. Major Achievements Ramesses III is best known for defeating the Sea Peoples, who had destroyed many ancient civilizations around the Mediterranean. His victories by land and sea protected Egypt from invasion and helped keep the kingdom independent.He also defeated the Libyans, who tried to invade Egypt from the west. Challenges Although Ramesses III won many battles, Egypt became poorer during his reign because of wars, droughts, and reduced trade. These problems weakened the kingdom after his death.

Ramesses II or Ramesses the Great was the 3rd known Pharaoh of the Nineteenth Dynasty of ancient Egypt, ruling from 1279 to 1213 BC (66 years). He is known for his military and architectural achievements. Archaeologists estimate that Ramesses II was born around 1303 to Pharaoh Seti I and Queen Tuya. Although he was counted as a Pharaoh of the Nineteenth Dynasty, he was not of royal blood with his grandfather Ramesses I having been previously been a Vizier and military officer and named heir to the throne by the previous Pharoah, as that Pharaoh had no blood born heir to their throne.
Military Achievements From as young as 14, Ramesses II would accompany his father on his military campaigns across Libya and the Levant areas including Israel, Syria, Lebanon, Jordan etc. By the age of 22, Ramesses was already running his own military campaigns across Nubia now modern day Sudan with his sons, and was later named co-ruler with Seti.
Under the Hittite king Suppiluliama I, Egypt had lost many trading centers in Canaan and Syria to the rival super power. Although his father Seti had previously recaptured these trading centers, they were taken back by the Hittite King Muwatalli II.
Upon ascending to the throne after his father’s death, Ramesses, by then estimated to be around 24 years old, began a military campaign to once again recapture and restore these trading centers along the borders of Egypt.

Throne Room III

The Pharaonic Throne of Ra the third

Rameses III had two primary wives and several secondary wives, and it was one of these secondary wives, Tiye, who ultimately caused his downfall. She hatched a plot to kill him and place her son, prince Pentaweret, on the throne. She and her confederates stirred up a rebellion and used magic wax images and poison as their weapons. The conspiracy failed and the traitors were arrested, but not before Rameses was fatally wounded. Fourteen officials sat in judgment and all the accused, with the exception one, was found guilty and condemned to commit suicide. Rameses died before the trial was completed. Rameses III’s death marks the end of an era. He had ruled for 31 years and was the last of the great Pharaohs. Egypt now suffered economic problems and could not exploit the revolution of the Iron Age (This began around 1200 BC) because she had no sources of ore. But the most important factor in Egypt’s decline was a breakdown in the fabric of society. There were disputes between officials and governors and infighting between the north and south. The priesthood became over powerful and eventually they took control of the government. From this time onwards, others would determine the destiny of the Mediterranean world. The Assyrians, Persians, Greeks and eventually the Romans were to become the lead players on the stage of international politics.
